Pulpit Vault

About this edition

Pulpit Vault is a digital reading edition of the Latter-day Saint preached record: 10,531 discourses spanning 1830–2026, from the earliest conference minutes of the restoration through the Journal of Discourses, the printed Conference Reports, and the modern published conference addresses. Every text is kept as a plain, citable document with its provenance recorded alongside it — where the words come from, how close they are to what was actually said, and what editorial work has been done to them.

This is an independent scholarly project. It is not an official resource of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, and nothing here carries the Church's endorsement. For the Church's own published texts, see the Gospel Library.

Scan links point to the specific page on which a talk begins, not merely to the volume. On talk pages, View scan opens the archival page image beside the transcript, so the reading text can be checked against its printed witness without leaving the page.

The fidelity rubric

Not every recorded sermon is a transcript. Fidelity grades state, on every talk, how close the surviving text is to what was actually said. When the evidence is uncertain, the policy is to grade down and say why. The grade appears beside every text as one of six marks:

●

Verbatim — the official word-for-word text.

◕

Authoritative print — an officially published record, lightly edited for print.

◑

Contemporaneous report — taken down at the time by a reporter or scribe; the speaker often never reviewed it.

◔

Reconstructed — editorially assembled from partial witnesses.

○

Fragmentary — only excerpts, summaries, or a partial record survive.

·

Unknown — an unsourced stub, kept visible rather than hidden.

The grade describes the text's relationship to the spoken sermon, never its importance. A journal summary of an 1843 discourse is graded honestly as fragmentary; it is not promoted into something it is not.

Editorial method and quality controls

Texts are transcribed from their sources conservatively: obvious scanning errors are corrected, printing artifacts (running heads, page numbers, broken hyphenation) are removed, and paragraphs are reflowed — but wording is never modernized, paraphrased, abridged, or improved. Printed editorial side-headings are kept as part of the record. Where a source is illegible, the edition says so rather than guessing.

Every talk then passes through explicit review stages, recorded on its full textual record page: a proofreading pass against the source text, and for the archival standard, a word-by-word collation against the original scan with the source, boundaries, speaker, date, and calling confirmed from evidence. Automated quality gates run over the whole corpus and block ingestion on missing provenance, unreflowed scanner text, and overclaimed editorial notes. Speaker identities are controlled against a single authority file, so that the archive's 1,100-plus speakers resolve to stable, deduplicated identities. An independent accuracy audit — random paragraphs diffed against the source scans, conference by conference — is in progress across the reviewed corpus.

Each page carries an edition line naming the exact revision of the underlying archive it was built from (this build: 24 July 2026, commit 34d0e94027). A citation to this edition therefore names a reproducible state of the text.

The corpus by era

Early restoration, 1830–1850

The earliest era is the sparsest: 37 records, hand-curated from the Joseph Smith Papers, Times and Seasons, the Church History Library, and related sources. Very few sermons from these decades survive as texts at all. What survives is often conference minutes, a clerk's summary, or notes in a hearer's journal — so this edition does not invent talk boundaries where the sources record none. Where only minutes survive, the document is presented honestly as a conference record; where a sermon survives only in partial notes, it is graded fragmentary or reconstructed, never verbatim. Supplied titles are marked as supplied. The result is a small shelf, but an honest one: each item states exactly what kind of witness it is.

Journal of Discourses and the post-JoD tail, 1850–1896

The legacy era directory currently contains 710 records, including conference records and post-JoD newspaper material through 1896. That number is not the size of theJournal of Discourses. The complete 26-volume printed catalog contains 1,426 entries and is being re-ingested entry by entry against its page scans. Its texts pose a distinctive problem: sermons were taken down in shorthand by reporters and reconstructed for print, and speakers often never reviewed the published result. This edition therefore grades JD texts as contemporaneous reports at best — never as authoritative print — and records the reporter where the printed header names one. The JD mixed general conference sermons with Sunday Tabernacle addresses, funerals, and special conferences. The new ingestion lane verifies each printed occasion, classifies it against the conference calendar, cites its precise BYU page, and checks backup transcript copies for omissions without treating those copies as authority. Progress is reported against 1,426, never against the legacy directory count.

Conference Reports, 1897–1970

The largest era: 5,530 talks from the printed Conference Report, the Church's official record of each general conference. These are officially published texts, lightly edited for print, and are graded accordingly as authoritative print. The editorial work of this era is largely typographic archaeology: the reports survive as two-column scans, and recovering clean reading text means reconstructing the column reading order, removing running heads and page numbers, repairing hyphenation, and reflowing paragraphs — while keeping the printed side-headings that structure many talks. Each talk links to the Internet Archive scan of its report, anchored to the page on which it begins, so the transcription can always be checked against the printed original.

Modern, 1971–present

The modern era's 4,254 addresses come from the Church's own published conference texts, which since 1971 have been issued in official word-for-word form. These talks are graded verbatim and cite the official text directly; there is no scan to reproduce, because the authority here is the published digital text itself, with web-archive captures recorded as insurance against link rot. For this era the edition's contribution is not textual recovery but continuity: the modern addresses sit in the same archive, under the same citation scheme and the same fidelity rubric, as the shorthand reports of the 1850s — one corpus, readable and comparable across196 years.

Citing this edition

Every talk page has a stable URL derived only from its identifier, a per-paragraph anchor (the ¶ mark that appears beside each paragraph), and a ready-made citation in its footer including the edition date and revision. If a review pass later changes a title or a source attribution, the URL does not move.
