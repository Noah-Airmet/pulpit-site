Historical introduction

Approximately eighty officers and other church members met near the Big Blue River in Jackson County, Missouri, on 6 April 1833 for instruction and worship. The account emphasizes the third anniversary of the church's organization and is a narrative report rather than a set of sermon transcripts. ¶

This material remains one conference record because the surviving sources preserve proceedings, retrospective summaries, or reported remarks rather than a set of discrete, complete sermon transcripts. ¶

Transcript

History, 1838–1856, volume A-1

6 April 1833 • Saturday

1On the 6th. of April, in the land of Zion which was within the western boundaries of the State of Missouri, about eighty official, together with some unofficial members of the church, met for instruction, and the service of God, at the Ferry on Big Blue river, [HC 1:336] near the western limits of Jackson County, which is the confines of the State and the United p. 283 ¶

States. It was an early spring, and the leaves and blossoms, like a glimpse at paradise, enlivened and gratified the soul of man. ¶

The day was spent in a very agreeable manner, in giving and receiving knowledge which appertained to this last kingdom. It being just 1800 years since the Savior laid down his life that men might have everlasting life, and only three years since the church had come out of the wilderness preparatory for the last dispensation, they had great reason to rejoice; They thought upon the time when this world came into existence, and the morning stars sang together, and all the sons of God shouted for joy: When Israel eat the “passover” as wailing came up for the loss of the first-born of Egypt, and they felt like the shepherds who watched their flocks by night, when the angelic choir sweetly sung that electrifying strain, “Peace on earth and good will to man,[”] and the solemnities of Eternity rested upon them. This was the first attempt made by the Church, to celebrate its her birth day, and those who professed not our faith talked about it as a strange thing. ¶
