Transcription status

This file preserves a manuscript transcription of Thomas Bullock's 26-leaf "Pottawattamie settlements, 1847 December 23-27" (CR 100 318), held by the Church History Library. The catalog identifies only December 24, 26, and 27 as Conference sessions. Those are the scope of this record; all 19 leaves that fall within that scope (1-5, 8-19) are now transcribed. ¶

The item also contains a December 23 meeting of the Twelve, December 25 High Council and Twelve council meetings, a General Epistle recipient list, and a music-donation list. They are deliberately left out below because they are adjacent council business or administrative appendices rather than conference proceedings. ¶

Use bracketed notes for uncertainty rather than smoothing or supplying missing words -- see heavyweight_review_notes above for which passages carry the most uncertainty (leaf 9 in particular). The secondary Brigham Young letter below corroborates the sustaining vote's outcome (names and offices match exactly) but remains a personal letter, not a substitute for the clerk's minutes. ¶

Brigham Young's account, from his letter to Orson Spencer

Source: Millennial Star, Vol. X, No. 8 (April 15, 1848), pp. 114-115. ¶

In December last we appointed a day to hold a conference on the other side of the river, in a large double block house, occupied by one of the brethren, where the Saints congregated in such large numbers that we found it impracticable to continue our conference, the house being so crowded and many shouting at the windows to get in, so that we adjourned for three weeks to build a house capable of holding the Saints. Accordingly, on the 24th, we convened again at the "Log Tabernacle," which they erected in a short time, during the severest weather we have had this winter. It is a well-constructed, capacious log house, 60 by 40 feet inside, and will seat 1000 persons, with a recess or stand 20 by 10 feet for the priesthood and a clerk's bench: it is certainly an ornament to this new country, and shows a little of Mormonism. I told them at the conference that the brethren had built, fenced, and made as many improvements in the short time they had been there (about a year) as they would in Missouri in about ten years; and it is a fact, and they have raised a crop equal to any we used to raise in Illinois. ¶

At this conference we suggested to the brethren the propriety of organizing the church with a first presidency and a patriarch, as hinted at in our General Epistle, and the expediency of such a move at this time was so clearly seen by the brethren, that they hailed it as an action which the state of the work at present demanded, and as a means to liberate the hands of the Quorum of the Twelve, who now feel at liberty to go abroad and herald the truth to the ends of the earth, and build up the kingdom in all the world. Accordingly Brigham Young was nominated to be the first president of the church, and he nominated Heber C. Kimball and Willard Richards to be his two counsellors, which nominations were seconded and carried without a dissentient voice. Father John Smith was then nominated to be patriarch of the whole church, in the same capacity as Father Joseph Smith was, and also Brother Hyrum -- seconded and carried unanimously. The Spirit of the Lord at this time rested upon the congregation in a powerful manner, insomuch that the Saints' hearts were filled with joy unspeakable; every power of their mind and nerve of their body was awakened and absorbed: a dead stillness reigned in the congregation while the President spoke. He said: -- ¶

"This is one of the happiest days of my life; it's according as Heber prophesied yesterday, our teachings to-day have been good. I never heard better. Is not the bliss of heaven and the breezes of Zion wafted here? Who feels hatred, malice, or evil? If you come to the door with a bad spirit, it would not come in with you; no, it could not mingle here: but when you enter, your feelings become as calm and gentle as the zephyrs of paradise; and I feel glory, Hallelujah. Nothing more has been done to-day than what I knew would be done when Joseph died. We have been driven from Nauvoo here, but the hand of the Lord is in it, -- visible as the sun shining this morning; it is visible to my natural eyes; it's all right: and I expect when we see the result of all we pass through in this probationary state, we will discover the hand of the Lord in it all, and shout Amen -- it's all right! We shall make the upper courts ring; we have something to do before then. I don't calculate to go beyond the bounds of time and space where we will have no opposition, -- no devils to contend with; and I have no fault to find with the providences of the Lord, nor much fault to find with the people; and if the devils keep out of my path I will not quarrel with them. As the Lord's will is my will all the time, as he dictates so I will perform. If he don't guide the ship, we'll go down in the whirlpool. Joseph told the Twelve, the year before he died, 'there is not one key or power to be bestowed on this church to lead the people into the celestial gate but I have given you, showed you, and talked it over to you; the kingdom is set up, and you have the perfect pattern, and you can go and build up the kingdom, and go in at the celestial gate, taking your train with you.'" ¶

The instrumental band was then called upon to perform, when the heavenly vibrations fell on the tender nerve of the ear, accompanied by the Spirit of God, and the Saints shouted, "Hosanna, Hosanna, Hosanna to God and the Lamb. Amen, Amen, and Amen!" led by Brother George A. Smith. The conference lasted four days. We had indeed an excellent time, and on the 13th January attended another meeting, convened by the seventies, which they called a Jubilee; but I told them it could not be considered a Jubilee spoken of in the Revelations, for all bonds were not broken, and I called it Jubilo, -- when the Saints assembled and spent the Sabbath in preaching and exhortation; and on Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, and Thursday, had preaching and teaching concerning the organization of companies for travelling westward, -- music, and other recreations. We had a blessed meeting -- all hearts were comforted and lifted up above our trials and persecutions, and went home rejoicing in the benefits and privileges of the liberty of the gospel of Jesus Christ; and I pray they may thereby be stimulated to pursue the path of righteousness, and fill up the remainder of their days in promoting the kingdom of peace and happiness on the earth. ¶

Collation notes

  • Young's letter dates the successful (second) meeting to "the 24th" of December and says the conference "lasted four days" (Dec. 24-27); the CHL manuscript's catalog description confirms this and adds Dec. 23 as an earlier meeting of the Twelve alone, before the full Conference opened in the Log Tabernacle on the 24th. The First Presidency sustaining vote is now confirmed to Dec. 27 specifically, matching indexes/conference-calendar.md's Dec. 25-27 window (which appears to undercount by starting two days after Young's letter's "24th").
  • The first, overcrowded meeting attempt ("in December last... on the other side of the river, in a large double block house") is described as happening before the Log Tabernacle meeting, with a three-week gap to build the Tabernacle; Young's letter does not give that first attempt's exact date.
  • This conference ratified the Twelve Apostles' Dec. 5, 1847 council decision (at Orson Hyde's house) to reorganize the First Presidency; see this vault's 1848-04-conference-record-log-tabernacle-conference.md and 1848-10-conference-record-great-salt-lake-valley-conference.md for the two later conferences (Council Bluffs, April 1848; Great Salt Lake Valley, October 1848) that, per Jenson's Church Chronology, ratified the same reorganization again for Saints not present at this one.
  • "Father John Smith" (uncle of Joseph Smith Sr.) was sustained here as Patriarch to the whole Church; this is corroborated by Jenson's Church Chronology, which separately notes John Smith "had been sustained as a 'Patriarch in the Church' as early as the general conference held at Winter Quarters, April 6, 1847" -- a full earlier conference this vault does not yet have a conference_record for.
  • The CHL manuscript's day-by-day description confirms Young's letter is describing the same conference this file records, and adds named speakers Young's letter does not mention (Wilford Woodruff, Orson Pratt, Amasa M. Lyman, William I. Appleby, Ezra T. Benson, John Young) across the full Dec. 23-27 run.
  • Now that the manuscript's own text is transcribed (leaves 1-5, 8-19), the clerk's minutes and Young's letter corroborate each other closely on the sustaining vote itself: both give Brigham Young as President, Heber C. Kimball and Willard Richards as counselors, and John Smith as Patriarch, with no dissenting vote. The manuscript adds detail the letter omits or compresses -- named seconds for each nomination (Orson Hyde nominating Young; William W. Major moving the motion, seconded by Henry W. Miller; O. H. Young and Phinehas H. Young seconding the counselor nominations; Heber C. Kimball seconding both Patriarch motions), Amasa Lyman's extended body-and-head argument for why the Twelve needed a First Presidency restored, and the organizational discussion earlier on Dec. 27 about consolidating the ~30-36 quorums of Seventy under a single presidency. The letter's quoted Brigham Young remarks ("This is one of the happiest days of my life...") do not appear verbatim in the manuscript at that point in the transcription; either Bullock's minutes paraphrase more heavily there or the quoted remarks come from a portion of Young's closing address whose wording diverges between the two witnesses -- flagged as an open question rather than resolved by preferring one witness over the other.
  • Leaf 9 (Dec. 26, William I. Appleby's "signs of the times" discourse) is the least certain leaf in the transcription -- dense, fast longhand covering many contemporary place names and figures (Jerusalem, the Ottoman Sultan, Rothschild, the Mexican-American War, European revolutions of 1847-48). A second reader collating against the leaf image would likely improve on several bracketed readings there.
