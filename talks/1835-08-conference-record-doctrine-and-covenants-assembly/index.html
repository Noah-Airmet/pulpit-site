Minutes of the August 1835 General Assembly on the Doctrine and Covenants

Conference record

1835 August General Conference·Whole Conference·1835-08-17

Source note

Preferred witness: Minutes, 17 August 1835, pages 98–106, accessed 23 May 2026. The JSP transcript preserves the general assembly that examined and accepted the 1835 Doctrine and Covenants, together with ordinations, blessings, testimonies, votes, and appended statements. ¶

The transcript below follows the Joseph Smith Papers expanded-transcription presentation: original spelling and punctuation are retained; editorial additions appear in brackets; insertions appear in angle brackets; cancellations appear with strikethrough; and manuscript page endings are linked to the corresponding JSP image and transcript page. ¶

Historical introduction

A general assembly convened in Kirtland on 17 August 1835 to consider the completed Book of Commandments and Covenants. The meeting included the organization of the assembly, ordinations and blessings, quorum-by-quorum testimony and voting, and approval of additional statements for inclusion in the volume. ¶

This material remains one conference record because the surviving source intermingles proceedings, reported remarks, and institutional business rather than preserving a set of discrete, complete sermon transcripts. ¶

  55. The Doctrine and Covenants version of these minutes includes the “written testimony of the Twelve.” Except for minor punctuation and spelling differences and nine word changes or additions, the text of this testimony is the same as the testimony prepared for the Book of Commandments, which was signed in 1831 by several church elders, including six who were called as members of the Twelve Apostles in 1835: Orson Hyde, William E. McLellin, Luke Johnson, Lyman Johnson, Parley P. Pratt, and Thomas B. Marsh. That testimony, as presented in this meeting, read: “The testimony of the witnesses to the book of the Lord’s commandments, which he gave to his church through Joseph Smith, jr. who was appointed by the voice of the church for this purpose: we therefore feel willing to bear testimony to all the world of mankind, to every creature upon the face of all the earth, and upon the islands of the sea, that the Lord has borne record to our souls, through the Holy Ghost shed forth upon us, that these commandments were given by inspiration of God, and are profitable for all men, and are verily true. We give this testimony unto the world, the Lord being our helper: and it is through the grace of God, the Father, and his Son Jesus Christ, that we are permitted to have this privilege of bearing this testimony unto the world, in the which we rejoice exceedingly, praying the Lord always, that the children of men may be profited thereby.” Since none of the Twelve were in Kirtland at this time, it is not clear when, or if, any members of the Twelve gave their assent to using that testimony as their testimony of the Doctrine and Covenants. After leaving the church, McLellin called the testimony printed in the Doctrine and Covenants “a base forgery” and indicated he did not approve of the 1835 edition of the Doctrine and Covenants because so many changes were made to the revelations. However, there is no record of McLellin voicing such disapproval in the 1830s.
