Temple building and temple worship

the house of the Lord, from Nauvoo's unfinished walls to a worldwide network

In conference discourse, temples move from urgent, unfinished communal projects to a worldwide system designed to put ordinances within reach. The thread also shows a changing emphasis: construction and sacrifice remain visible, but access, repeated worship, family history, and covenant keeping increasingly define what the buildings are for.

1850190019502000
  1. The early restoration · 1830–1850

  2. 1841October General Conferencefragmentary

    Baptism for the dead and the Nauvoo temple

    Conference record

    This fragmentary conference record ties baptism for the dead to a physical font in the Lord's House: Joseph Smith suspends the practice until it can be performed there. Brigham Young then asks elders to remain in Nauvoo and build, treating construction labor as equal to preaching abroad.

  3. The Journal of Discourses era · 1851–1896

  4. 1853April General Conferencecontemporaneous report

    Temples and the endowment

    Brigham Young · President of the Church

    At the laying of the Salt Lake Temple's southeast cornerstone, Young places the project in a sequence running from biblical tabernacles through Kirtland and Nauvoo. He also gives a concrete definition of the endowment and makes the unfinished building necessary to receiving it.

  5. 1873October General Conferencecontemporaneous report

    Why temples, and where

    Orson Pratt · Member of the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles

    Pratt inventories the temple's distinctive work—washings, anointings, sealings, baptism for the dead, and record keeping—rather than treating it as another meetinghouse. His closing call for temples in northern and southern Utah, so older Saints need not travel six hundred miles, anticipates the later logic of distributed access.

  6. 1893April General Conferenceauthoritative print

    Salt Lake Temple Dedicatory Prayer

    Wilford Woodruff · President of the Church

    The Salt Lake Temple dedicatory prayer marks the completion of the building begun forty years earlier. Its detailed petitions for ordinance workers, recorders, genealogists, and work connecting living and dead turn a monumental construction achievement into an operating program.

  7. The Conference Reports · 1897–1970

  8. 1900October General Conferenceauthoritative print

    Vicarious Work for the Dead

    Rudger Clawson · Of the Council of the Twelve Apostles

    Clawson insists that a temple's value lies not in architecture or expense but in ordinances for the living and dead. He presents temple service as a field of useful labor for people unable to serve missions and explicitly includes women in that work.

  9. 1917April General Conferenceauthoritative print

    Temples, expenditures, and ordinance scale

    Joseph F. Smith · General Authority

    Smith's institutional report records construction spending in Cardston and Hawaii alongside 419,257 temple ordinances performed in 1916—then the Church's annual high. The juxtaposition captures both geographic expansion and the new administrative scale of temple work.

  10. 1955October General Conferenceauthoritative print

    Why the Latter-day Saints Build Temples

    Bruce R. McConkie · Of the First Council of the Seventy

    Speaking as the Swiss temple opened, McConkie says temple blessings should be readily available wherever congregations are large enough. He distinguishes temples from ordinary worship spaces and centers them on ordinances, covenants, and continued faithfulness rather than architecture alone.

  11. The modern record · 1971–present

  12. 1985October General Conferenceverbatim

    Rejoice in This Great Era of Temple Building

    Gordon B. Hinckley · Second Counselor in the First Presidency

    Hinckley reports sixteen dedications in little more than two and a half years and states the governing aim plainly: take temples closer to the people. His accounts from Manila, Freiberg, Johannesburg, and elsewhere present temple building as a marker of an increasingly international church.

  13. 1998April General Conferenceverbatim

    New Temples to Provide “Crowning Blessings” of the Gospel

    Gordon B. Hinckley · President of the Church

    After describing members traveling for days by inexpensive buses and boats, Hinckley announces about thirty smaller temples and a goal of one hundred operating temples by century's end. The small-temple program changes the scale and speed at which access can expand.

  14. 2000April General Conferenceverbatim

    A Temple for West Africa

    Glenn L. Pace · Of the Seventy

    Pace makes unequal access measurable: among 85,000 members in West Africa, he says only 400 had received the endowment because reaching Johannesburg or London was prohibitively expensive. The announced Accra temple appears here as infrastructure needed for a mature regional church, not simply as a symbol.

  15. 2008October General Conferenceverbatim

    Holy Temples, Sacred Covenants

    Silvia H. Allred · First Counselor in the Relief Society General Presidency

    Allred remembers a 1976 Central American temple trip that required five days of travel each way and six border crossings, then contrasts it with temples in Costa Rica and Panama and one announced in Honduras. She uses that history of access to explain the ordinances, preparation, and repeated worship the buildings enable.

  16. 2021April General Conferenceverbatim

    COVID-19 and Temples

    Russell M. Nelson · President of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints

    This report documents the first worldwide temple closure and a four-phase reopening governed by local health conditions. Even amid interrupted worship, Nelson notes forty-one temples under construction or renovation and announces twenty more, framing continued expansion as preparation for future access.

  17. 2024April General Conferenceverbatim

    Temples, Houses of the Lord Dotting the Earth

    Neil L. Andersen · Of the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles

    Andersen describes 182 operating temples and uses the Philippines—eleven more temples then being built, designed, or prepared—as a case study in network density. The argument has shifted from whether distant regions will receive a temple to how frequent local worship is meant to shape discipleship.
